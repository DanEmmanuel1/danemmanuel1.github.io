Creepy-crawlies splash page!

In casual language, people use "bugs" to mean all sorts of small creepy-crawlies: insects, spiders, etc. However, "Bugs" (true bugs) are actually just one order of insects (Hemiptera). This page provides a summary of the main classes or groups of creepy-crawlies.

Beetles

Beetles are a group of insects that have hardened forewings, or elytra, that serve as protective wing cases for their hindwings. They have a hard exoskeleton and chew their food. They make up the largest group of animals on Earth.

Photo: Jonas Bergsten via Flickr Creative Commons.

True bugs

True bugs are a group of insects whose front wings are thickened at the base and membranous at the tip. They have sucking mouthparts and are mostly plant-feeders. They are named after the insect order Hemiptera (from the Greek Hemi + ptera, meaning "half wing").

Photo: Mickael via Flickr Creative Commons.

Butterflies and moths

These insects have two pairs of large, often colorful wings. They have a sucking mouthpart called a proboscis. The larvae of butterflies and moths are called caterpillars. They undergo complete metamorphosis.

Photo: Jeff Gunn via Flickr Creative Commons.

Flies and mosquitos

These insects have just one pair of wings. They have sucking mouthparts and undergo complete metamorphosis. The larvae are called maggots.

Photo: Callan Bentley via Flickr Creative Commons.

Bees, wasps, and ants

These insects have a narrow "waist" between the thorax and abdomen. They have two pairs of wings (except for worker ants, which are wingless). They undergo complete metamorphosis.

Photo: Paolo Barzano via Flickr Creative Commons.

Spiders

Spiders are arachnids, not insects. They have eight legs and fangs that can inject venom. They have simple eyes and most make silk. Unlike other arachnids, they are almost all carnivorous.
